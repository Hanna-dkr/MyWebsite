Back to case studies Designing a design system: ComUnity Developer Toolkit

As a senior UX consultant at Pondo, I was brought in to help expand and embed consistent UX practice within the ComUnity Developer Toolkit team. Once onboard, I uncovered significant design and technical debt that was slowing development and creating friction across the workflow.

To address this, I championed and led the creation of a scalable design system—turning fragmented legacy designs into a shared framework that enabled faster delivery, better collaboration, and long-term UX consistency.

Objective

Embedding user-centred design into the product—and the process

ComUnity Developer Toolkit is a low-code platform for rapidly building custom digital tools. The product’s complexity—with its multi-step developer workflows—had grown over time, while UX input remained minimal. As the platform scaled, this led to increasing usability issues, inconsistencies, and delivery friction. In response, ComUnity brought in Pondo for long-term UX support.

I joined the existing Pondo team to expand their UX capacity—embedding more deeply within the product team to support scalable, sustainable design. Once onboard, it became clear that design was not yet integrated into the team workflow.

I inherited a fragmented Figma file created through years of isolated design efforts - it contained 365 screens, 100+ undocumented components and an outdated style guide. Without naming conventions or reusable structures, patterns were inconsistently applied and hard to maintain. This made handoff inefficient, introduced recurring friction, and blocked efforts to scale design efficiently.

My goal was to create a design system that bridged design and development, reduced technical debt, and laid the foundation for consistent, user-centred design. This case study focuses on that effort—part of a broader initiative by Pondo to bring clarity and structure to UX delivery.

Above: The unstructured Figma file I inherited was blocking efforts to scale design efficiently.

My role in the team

Building design foundations in a developer-led team

As a Senior UX Consultant at Pondo, I worked alongside our UX Lead to expand design capacity on the ComUnity team. While the UX Lead focused on strategy, I was embedded day-to-day in a developer-led product team—collaborating with a product manager (also acting as CTO and product marketing lead), a delivery manager, and an engineering team with one front-end developer.

My role was to integrate design into the team’s delivery workflow and establish a scalable foundation for consistent, user-centred design.

This included:

  • Leading the creation and maintenance of a scalable design system in Figma
  • Advocating for the design system’s value to both technical and non-technical stakeholders
  • Promoting user-centred interaction patterns and inclusive design across a complex, developer-focused product
  • Collaborating closely with developers to align design and implementation
  • Conducting informal validation of the design system with peer developers to ensure usability and relevance

Within this setup, I was responsible for translating design goals into practical systems—helping the team move from ad-hoc decisions to shared standards that improved both speed and quality.

Organisation diagram indicating key decision makers in the developer-lead product team.

Constraints and challenges

Balancing foundational work with fast-paced delivery

Building a design system in a high-velocity, developer-led environment came with several constraints. UX was welcomed in principle, but not yet embedded in process or resourced for long-term impact. While balancing hands-on execution with systems thinking, I navigated the following conditions:

Feature delivery was a priority
  • The team was initially cautious about whether a design system would support or slow delivery
  • We worked from existing personas and earlier in-house research, as no new user research was commissioned during this phase
  • Design was valued, but dedicating time to process required advocacy—and was often deprioritised in favour of delivery speed.
Managing legacy design debt
  • I inherited a large, unstructured Figma file—365 screens and 108 components with no documentation or naming conventions
  • The current implementation did not consider responsivity or accessibility
  • Figma was the sole design tool, requiring all systemisation, documentation, and collaboration to happen within that environment
Limited capacity, fast-paced setting
  • The design system was developed in parallel with ongoing feature work, without a dedicated sprint
  • Time and budget were limited, and the front-end team was lean
  • As an enterprise-grade, highly configurable platform, the product required a flexible, scalable approach to design patterns

The approach

Laying the groundwork for scalable design

The design system needed to support fast-paced delivery without slowing the team down. To make this possible, we focused on gaining buy-in, working collaboratively, and laying just enough structure to guide consistent, user-centred design—without requiring a full overhaul of the UI.

Above: We mapped desired outcomes and existing barriers, then pinpointed interventions to bridge the gap.

1
Framing the design system as a shared tool for improving efficiency:

I positioned the design system as a way to reduce reliance on mockups, clarify implementation, and support faster onboarding—framing it as a scalable, low-friction solution to improve quality without slowing delivery.

2
Focusing on structured, incremental improvements:

I began by auditing the existing UI to identify common, high-impact patterns and inconsistencies. The focus was on foundational elements that could deliver immediate value without slowing the team down.

3
Recognising that adoption was key, we brought developers into the process early:

I held walkthroughs with developers to align on structure and usage, asked how components were built and reused in code, and used their feedback to validate the system’s logic and naming conventions.

4
Making Figma the Single Source of Truth:

With Figma as our sole tool, we built a centralised, flexible system that supported both design and development. We applied atomic principles, used interactive states to show behaviour, and structured files for clarity and reuse. Embedded documentation guided usage, making Figma a shared, evolving source of truth across the team.

Execution

Heading

The audit: Understanding the foundation before rebuilding
  • I audited 365 screens and 108 components from the legacy Figma file—spotting inconsistencies in spacing, typography, colour use, and interaction patterns.
  • A workshop with the product owner confirmed that the styling had been driven by brand colours rather than by usability.
  • An accessibility review showed that key UI elements like buttons and navigation didn’t meet WCAG contrast standards. There were also no component states or focus states, with few broader inclusive design measures in place.
  • I revisited the existing personas and early research that had shaped the original styling—looking for useful insights we could carry forward.
  • I also spoke with the engineers to understand their frustrations. The biggest one? Not knowing which components or patterns to use—especially when it came to displaying information or giving system feedback.

Above: After reviewing the existing user personas as part of the audit process, I documented insights and created a set of design principles to help us move forward.

Communicating issues and advocating for user-centred processes

We shared the audit findings with stakeholders and proposed a Phase 1 design system focused on documenting core components and patterns - we got buy-in to move forward.

Above: Excerpts from the presentation I gave at the accessibility workshop, where we made a proposal to incrementally improve accessibility.

  • In an accessibility workshop, I highlighted WCAG basics and made the case for designing with edge-case users in mind. Colour contrast was a key issue—brand colours used in navigation and buttons didn’t meet standards. I suggested tweaks that stayed true to the brand while improving readability.
  • We got buy-in to adjust key colours immediately and include broader accessibility updates in the future roadmap

Results

What happened next?

NOTHING...

Nothing substantial happened for seven years.

The project was paused in 2015 after the Trading Plan was submitted for approval, due to issues with budget allocation. In 2016, the City adopted a new Transit Oriented Development Strategy. They could revisit the project and included it in an application for grant funding, which was approved in 2019.

In 2021, the City returned to SUN Development and contracted the organisation to re-audit the traders and update the concept plans, with the intention of implementing in 2022.

I followed up on the project outcome in April 2025 - a tender process had been initiated, indicating progress toward implementation. SUN Development was actively involved, working to keep traders and leaders engaged.

But something, actually...

Despite the slow pace, the project delivered meaningful results.

  • The stakeholder engagement process itself was a win. It shifted the conversation from “compliance” to “support.” This was shown by the City's endorsement of the Trading Plan, indicating a policy-level shift—recognising complexity.
  • The City’s return to SUN Development after years of inactivity showed that the original work had value. That decision—to pick up the project and re-engage traders—points to longer-term institutional learning.

In short: not a transformation, but a recalibration. Small shifts that move us closer to re-making South African cities—safer, more inclusive, and more honest about the inequalities they were built on.

Lessons

What I learned from this project

1

Proximity reveals complexity

Working closely with traders dismantled convenient binaries—formal/informal, legal/illegal, public/private. They weren’t operating outside the system; they were a key reason it was able to function. This project pushed me to look beyond surface symptoms and see how deeply rooted systems shape what we too easily frame as ‘problems.’

2

Listening shifts power - and changes what gets built

This project taught me to recognise the depth of lived expertise—knowledge shaped by experience, not credentials. Listening to traders wasn’t just a method for collecting insights; it was a way to make space for them to define their own realities. In a system that often sidelines or speaks over them, being heard carried real weight. As someone involved in shaping outcomes, I came to understand that listening isn’t neutral—it redistributes influence. When the people most affected help shape the story, the outcomes are more grounded—and they work better.

3

Participation can come at a cost - especially for the vulnerable

After working closely with traders over two years, I’ve kept coming back to the trust they placed in us. They shared insight, lived experience and time—often during their working hours. They were willing participants, knowing that the outcome of the project could meaningfully affect their livelihoods. Although we were transparent about the uncertainties around implementation, I sometimes wonder if the process asked too much of them, especially given the long delays that followed. This experience reminded me that incentivisation in design isn’t just about tokens or stipends. It’s about understanding what’s at stake for the people involved, and taking responsibility for the weight of their trust—especially when outcomes are slow, partial, or unclear.

4

Facilitating inclusive engagement

Facilitating participatory workshops taught me how to create space for different voices—especially those often excluded from formal processes. It required active listening, flexibility, and the ability to guide discussion without dominating it. Working closely with the community facilitator and translator was essential—not just for communication, but for building trust and cultural understanding. Through this, I learned how to surface tensions respectfully, balance competing needs, and keep conversations focused and meaningful, even when the topics were complex or emotionally charged.

Designing systems, not just screens.

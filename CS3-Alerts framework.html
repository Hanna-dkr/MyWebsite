Back to case studies Aligning alerting with Developer mental models

In 2024, ComUnity set out to ship observability alerting as part of an MVP—within three months. As a Senior UX Consultant at Pondo, I designed a scalable alerts framework and defined clear, consistent messaging patterns. The challenge was aligning with developer mental models—supporting awareness without disrupting flow. The MVP shipped on time, laying the groundwork for future growth.

Objective

We set out to design a scalable alerts experience that matched how developers think and work.

ComUnity Developer Toolkit is a low-code platform that helps developers quickly build and deploy custom tools. To improve transparency, the team planned to introduce alerting with a fast turnaround—focusing solely on observability.

While this limited implementation made sense from a delivery perspective, we saw the risk of creating design debt —introducing patterns that might not scaleor could lead to a fragmented user experience as more alerts were added over time.

We proposed developing an alerting framework up front: a consistent approach to how alerts would be surfaced, structured, and understood across the platform.

Our objective was to understand how developer users engage with event information—what they need to know, when they need to know it, and how to present that information in a way that supports, rather than interrupts, their workflow. The goal was a clear, scalable alerts framework that balanced visibility, clarity, and minimal disruption.

We created this experience flow to map user needs alongside system processes and clarify where key interactions should occur.

My role in the team

End-to-end UX delivery in a lean product environment

As a Senior UX Consultant at Pondo, I joined the ComUnity Developer Toolkit team in early 2023. I worked alongside our UX Lead—who steered overall strategy—while I was responsible for leading the design and delivery of the alert framework.

Product roadmap from the product owner, outlining how alerting fit within the broader plan for rapid feature delivery.

The product team was lean and engineering-led, consisting of a product manager (also acting as CTO and product marketing lead), a delivery manager, and a six person engineering team.

My role included:
  • Advocating for upfront research and testing, then doing secondary research when new user research was out of scope
  • Mapping developer needs through internal feedback and platform observation
  • Facilitating workshops to align on goals and terminology
  • Iterating on the design framework through collaborative reviews with peer developers to ensure usability and relevance
  • Supporting developer hand-off and implementation
  • Managing and updating the design system to support alert patterns

Constraints & project risks

Without fresh research, we risked missing the mark on user needs.

The existing user research was outdated

We had access to legacy user research and personas but it had become outdated as the Toolkit evolved. It didn't cover how real users worked and thought about tasks.

New research was removed from our scope

With no time or budget for formal research, we relied on secondary sources and informal interviews to understand how developers think.

The risk: Misunderstanding user mental models

There was a real risk that our conceptual model wouldn't match user mental models and we would design an alerting system that didn’t match how developers actually work.

The approach

Practical steps to bridge the gap between system events and UX

To deliver an alerts framework that aligned with developer mental models—and avoided design debt—we focused on four key work-streams:

1
Understanding the users without direct research
  • We flagged the outdated user research as a risk and proposed a lean research plan for inclusion in the three-month delivery cycle. When research was ruled out, we turned to secondary methods to understand developer needs.
  • I drew from existing personas and academic research on Developer Experience and tooling.
  • I spoke with internal engineers to uncover pain points and assumptions—while keeping in mind they weren’t representative users.
  • I reviewed enterprise platforms and open-source design systems to identify relevant patterns and guide our approach.

2
Planning for system-wide alerting
  • We began by mapping alert and notification needs across the platform, cataloguing potential events and identifying where user touch points would be required.
  • We outlined the need for an alerts prioritisation scale.
  • We aligned with engineers to ensure the framework fit backend constraints.
  • We aligned on an approach that balanced technical feasibility with user needs.

With the framework in place we focused on designing for the MVP
  • We defined design principles grounded in our research insights.

  • I designed and refined the alert interfaces through iteration, focusing on how users engage with and manage alerts effectively.
  • Internal engineers gave useful feedback on iterations—helpful, though not a substitute for real users.
  • I created content and interaction guidelines for how alerts should behave

Execution

1. Audit: Understanding the foundation before rebuilding

Above: After reviewing the existing user personas as part of the audit process, I documented insights and created a set of design principles to help us move forward.

  • Catalogued inconsistencies:

    Audited 365 screens and 108 components - Inconsistencies in spacing, typography, colour use, and interaction patterns were catalogued.

  • Learned that UI styling had been driven by brand colours:

    This was discovered through a styling alignment workshop with the product owner.

  • Key UI elements didn't meet WCAG AA contrast standards

    I conducted an accessibility review. Buttons and navigation were not designed to meet readability standards. There were also no component states or focus states, with few broader inclusive design measures in place.

  • I revisited the existing personas and early research:

    I put together a set of design principles to help us move forward. This was based on insights from the existing research.

  • I spoke with the engineers to understand their pain points:

    The biggest one? Not knowing which components or patterns to use—especially when it came to displaying information or giving system feedback.

2. Communicating issues and advocating for user-centred processes

Above: Excerpts from the presentation I gave at the accessibility workshop, where we made a proposal to incrementally improve accessibility.

  • We shared the audit findings with stakeholders:

    We proposed a Phase 1 design system focused on documenting core components and patterns - we got buy-in to move forward.

  • We got buy-in to proceed with incremental accessibility improvements:

    In an accessibility workshop, I highlighted WCAG standards and made the case for designing with edge-case users in mind. Colour contrast was a key issue—brand colours used in navigation and buttons didn’t meet standards. We got support to adjust key colours immediately and include broader accessibility updates in the future roadmap

3. Structuring scalable components

  • I created a structure around core foundations:

    A system for colour, typography and spacing were set up using Figma variables, incorporating accessibility improvements. An icon library was set up.

  • UI elements were created from the ground up:

    Atoms (buttons, inputs), molecules (form fields, nav items), and organisms (headers, sidebars)

Above: I used Figma variables to set up a foundation for the design system, allowing control and flexibility.

  • I structured reusable, scalable components around key interface elements

    I focused on navigation, modals, and core UI patterns—using clear naming conventions and Figma variants to show interactive states.

  • The Figma design system became the central hub for both design and engineering:

Lessons

Take aways from this project

  • Expertise helped—but it also got in the way:

    Pondo’s background in enterprise tools gave stakeholders confidence, which ironically made it harder to argue for research. But experience can only take you so far. Without real validation, you risk designing for assumptions instead of people. This project reminded me that even when you think you know the user, there’s no substitute for talking to them.

  • You can’t design what you don’t understand:

    Alerting and observability are deeply technical. Embedding with engineers helped me understand backend structures and constraints—which shaped the framework and made sure it was actually feasible.

  • Mental models matter more than clean UI:

    Naming friction—what counts as an "event" vs. an "alert"—surfaced early. It was a reminder that a polished interface means little if it doesn't match how users conceptualise the system.

  • Information architecture means understanding system behaviour:

    Before thinking about screens or UI, we had to understand how the system worked, when events occurred, and what users needed to know at each point. This shaped the logic and placement of alerts throughout the product.

  • Close Collaboration with Engineers Improved the Outcome:

    Working closely with the engineering team made the alerts framework stronger. Their insight helped refine the model, close knowledge gaps, and make design decisions more grounded.

Designing systems, not just screens.

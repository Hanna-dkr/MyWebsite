Empowering Informal Traders - A Strategy for Inclusive Design

In 2013, the Municipality of Cape Town targeted Nyanga’s overcrowded and unsafe transport hub for regeneration.

As part of a multidisciplinary, non-profit consortium, I played a key role in user-centered research focusing on informal traders—an essential yet overlooked stakeholder group.

My work included ethnographic research, systems analysis, stakeholder engagement and participatory design facilitation - it surfaced critical insights, shaping a regeneration strategy grounded in inclusion, safety, and economic resilience..

Why This Case Study Matters

From architectural systems thinking to inclusive UX design

This project represents one of many similar initiatives over eight years with SUN Development and VPUU, where I developed skills in systems thinking, human-centred design, user research, collaboration, and navigating complex environments.

Through ethnographic research and participatory facilitation, I translated user needs into actionable design principles, showcasing my ability to effectively understand users through research and design within challenging contexts.

Why was an upgrade commissioned?

Public safety, unregulated trade, and 7,000 daily commuters: A high-pressure urban hub in need of a better system

Above-left: Aerial view of the interchange at the heart of the project site. Above-right: Trading conditions on site. Photos credit: SUN Development

Located in Nyanga, a densely populated and economically challenged neighbourhood, the transport hub faced significant issues with crime, congestion, and unregulated informal trade. Informal traders contributed to friction and sidewalk overcrowding.

The municipality required a safer, more structured public environment. Our team at SUN Development was tasked with designing a comprehensive plan to improve infrastructure and public safety, that included a trading strategy.

My role in the team

Researching the trading network and translating findings into inclusive design principles

As an architect on the team, I played a key role in executing research, synthesizing findings, and co-developing strategy—critical to driving a multidisciplinary urban regeneration approach. This involved:

  • Leading interviews with traders, conducting qualitative and quantitative field research, and facilitating participatory sessions
  • Collaborating with community facilitators and leading interns during on-site research
  • Mapping stakeholder needs and pain points across diverse user groups
  • Producing artefacts—maps, reports, infographics—to communicate insights
  • Collaborating with the urban design lead to create design principles that would inform the trading plan and regeneration strategy

Project stakeholder map: I engaged with community and government stakeholders (shown in grey) during a collaborative research and design process.

The objective

The goal was to create a trading plan that supported informal traders.

Achieving this meant developing a deep understanding of how trade functioned, what traders needed, and how an urban upgrade could help or harm their livelihoods. Our intention was to design for inclusion and long-term resilience.

The strategy

A participatory, systems-based approach to research and design.

Photo of co-design session with traders, including myself—evidence of a participatory, inclusive process. Photo credit: SUN Development

1. Gain an understanding of the status quo:

I worked closely with community facilitators and team members to conduct qualitative and quantitative field research within a systems thinking framework.

2. Participatory concept design:

Collaborating with the urban design lead, I developed initial trader-specific principles based on the research insights and facilitated workshops to refine them through direct stakeholder feedback.

3. Inclusive design principles to guide the final deliverables:

I translated the principles into spatial recommendations for the team to use in finalising deliverables, ensuring the final trading plan reflected both strategic insight and lived reality.

Navigating complexity

Design under pressure: Trust, language, and institutional silos

Building trust:

Challenge: Our link to the municipality initially created skepticism. Language barriers and the presence of police escorts added tension, with the risk of skewing research.

Remedy: Close collaboration with community facilitators was key to build trust and ensure informed participation.

(Photo: Policeman escort shadowing research on site)

Inclusive communication:

Challenge: Using conventional architectural methods to communicate ideas during participatory design sessions excluded participants who were not trained in the field.

Remedy: We evolved our methods to ensure participants were able to contribute and co-create - we used walkabouts, storytelling and physical models, encouraging physical involvement and creative expression.

(Photo: Co-design with traders. Credit - SUN Development)

Policy fragmentation:

Challenge: Decision making on integrated solutions was slowed down by municipal silos with conflicting agendas. Existing policy was also a barrier at times.

Remedy: We brought key stakeholders together in varying combinations to discuss challenges and possible solutions. Whenever possible we encouraged meeting on site, where the project was tangible.

(Photo: Problem solving meeting with City stakehlders. Credit - SUN Development)

The Research Process

Immersive fieldwork and multi‑stakeholder engagement

1
Understanding trade in context
  • We began with a review of municipal data to understand the area's socio-economic landscape and spatial systems.
  • Observational fieldwork:We studied the daily rhythm of informal trade in relation to commuter flows, movement patterns, infrastructure, and friction points.
  • Stakeholder walkabouts:We engaged with traders, residents, municipal officials, and law enforcement to hear firsthand accounts of the area’s challenges.

Gathering stories on site walkabouts. Photo: SUN Development.

2
I led a focused investigation into how informal trade functioned on the ground
  • Mapping daily operations:I guided the fieldwork process, working with two interns to map 365 trader stalls and gather quantitative data around location, typology, operations, and spatial needs.
  • Interviewing traders to gather qualitative data: I created interview scripts. We conducted 20 in-depth interviews with a representative sample of traders from different groups. I led the analysis of findings, identifying key themes around business structure, pain points, and daily practices.
  • Quantitative data integration: I reviewed the trader census data gathered by a separate team within our oganisation and used it to enrich our understanding .

Policy & precedent review
  • Municipal by-laws: We interviewed city officials and reviewed the applicable by-laws to understand friction points and areas of trader non-compliance.

The discovery

Traders provided an essential service to the broad community and desperately needed support

1
Traders filled a vital gap where formal retail fell short

The Trading network made the area a regional shopping destination, offering affordable goods in small quantities.

Only one formal mall was available in the area, to support 58 000 people (Census 2011).

2
Trade supported livelihoods well beyond the market itself

Traders relied on informal supply chains to supply their goods and services.

Trade sustained the local economy and provided vital jobs in an area with high unemployment.

3
Trade was a legitimate, life-long career for many

The community saw trading as a legitimate occupation.

52% of the 365 audited traders had worked from the same kiosk for over 11 years —some had been there for more than 35 years.

4
Trade was self-organised and cooperative

Traders had organised themselves into groups, based on the products they sold. They had systems in place for sharing space and resources, and overcoming challenges. For example, some groups shared the cost of hired security guards.

(Below: I created this map of organized trader groups by location, product, and customer interface—used to convey trade nuances in stakeholder sessions with the City.)

(Below: I also created a series of detailed site maps that were used to advocate for trader needs in discussions with the City.)

5
Infrastructure was absent, and conditions unsafe
"There are lots of skollies (thieves)… We try to protect each other and hired a security guard together. We don’t have any storage—it’s a problem. Anything left in the stall at night gets stolen."
– Female meat trader, Nyanga

Traders needed support—they lacked storage, water, waste disposal, toilets, and lighting.

Theft and harassment were common - women traders faced elevated risk—traders helped us to identify crime hots in the area.

6
Traders feared relocation, with good reason

Traders had carefully located themselves where there was customer availability.

Moving meant losing access to their customers and severing decades of established practices and cooperative working relationships.

7
Policy did not reflect reality

In 2013, the City limited informal trading to one permit per household, tied to a single demarcated space. Food traders also needed a certificate of acceptability.

But on the ground, ownership was complex—stalls were often shared by extended families, run through partnerships, or sublet entirely. Traders had varied space and service needs, but with little infrastructure or policy flexibility, compliance was near impossible—even as their trade met essential gaps in an under-serviced community.

(Above: An extended-family business - Ownership of stalls was complex and compliance was difficult.)

The risks posed by an urban upgrade

Overlooking the trading ecosystem would jeopardise both livelihoods and the local retail economy

Informal trade in Nyanga emerged to meet unmet community needs and played a vital role despite major challenges. Our research showed that top-down upgrades, if misaligned with how trade actually worked, risked displacing traders, disrupting supply chains, and weakening the local economy.

Design principles

Putting traders at the heart of the upgrade

Informed by field research and co‑design sessions, I collaborated with the urban design lead to developed six core design principles. These guided a trading plan proposal that was inclusive, practical, and sensitive to local dynamics.

A phased upgrade prioritising continuity

Phase construction to allow continuous operation.

Accommodate all existing traders and allow for future growth.

Minimize relocation and protect livelihoods

Only relocate traders when essential.

Co‑design new locations to improve conditions, foot traffic, and growth.

A central market as a destination

Create a high visibility destination market-place at the interchange.

Opportunity to provide shared infrastructure for food and meat traders and those needing relocation.

Design for safety, dignity, and support

Deliver lighting, designated pedestrian zones, and passive surveillance.

Match storage, waste, and water servicing to each trader group's needs.

Reflect informal reality in policy

Propose shared ownership options as part of the trading plan.

Propose demarcated bay sizes that realistically cater to needs.

Make community partnership ongoing

Establish feedback loops throughout implementation

Reframe the city’s role as partner, not just regulator

Outcomes & Lessons

Designing with people, not for them

1

Listening as learning

The traders we worked with had deep, lived expertise—built over decades. No framework or formal process could match their understanding of daily operations and needs.

2

Working across difference

Our team and the community came from different cultural backgrounds. This required curiosity, self‑awareness, and humility—beyond mere “empathy.”

3

Trust, hope—and hard questions

When people share their time, insight, and trust, they’re investing in a possible future. Designers carry a responsibility that extends long beyond the proposal stage.

4

A delayed return

Even years later, the City returned to build on our work—proof that genuine, people‑centered research lays foundations for long‑term impact.

Before diving into the details of case study take a moment to meet Nontsikelelo...

Nontsikelelo works as a vegetable trader in Nyanga.

It’s late afternoon and she's preparing to pack up her stall after a long, stressful day.

Today was really tough for her.

She nearly had her goods confiscated by a police officer because she forgot to bring her permit with her. She was fined instead. Her heart still races thinking about it.

This couldn't have come at a worse time—just last week, she was robbed, losing much of her earnings and stock.

The memory still haunts her, and her sleep has suffered ever since.

Now, as evening approaches, anxiety creeps in. The local thugs know traders like her carry cash, and they see older women as easy targets.

Her ankles are swollen after standing all day - at 52 years old, she is starting to feel the long days.

The prospect of carrying heavy bags of unsold vegetables the 3-kilometer journey home fills her with dread. It’s not a safe walk and she is tired.

Despite these hardships, Nontsikelelo keeps working hard every day, hoping to reach her goals...

This stall has been her spot for the past 20 years—her pride, her livelihood, and her source of strength.

It's allowed her to feed her family, pay for her child's tertiary education, and even save toward her dream of retiring in her rural hometown on the Transkei coast.

This case study explores how I researched the lived experiences of traders like Nontsikelelo, surfacing actionable insights that shaped a regeneration plan grounded in inclusion, safety, and economic resilience.

-I did this work as part of SUN Development- a multi-disciplinary non-profit organisation. The urban regeneration project was commissioned by the City of Cape Town and ran between 2013-2015.

Why was an upgrade commissioned?

Public safety, unregulated trade, and 7,000 daily commuters: A high-pressure urban hub in need of a better system

Located in Nyanga, one of Cape Town’s most densely populated and economically challenged neighborhoods, the site faced persistent problems with crime, overcrowding, and unregulated informal trade. Hundreds of informal traders operated daily, playing a vital role in the local economy—but also contributing to sidewalk congestion and regulatory friction with the City.

From the municipality’s perspective,this created a need for a safer, more organized public space that balanced enforcement with economic support. Our team, SUN Development, was commissioned to design a comprehensive regeneration plan, including a trading strategy that supported the informal economy rather than displacing it.

Project objective

The goal was to create a trading plan that supported—not displaced—informal traders.

Achieving this meant developing a deep understanding of how trade functioned, what traders needed, and how urban upgrades could help or harm their livelihoods. Our task was to design for inclusion and long-term resilience.

The discovery

Trade was essential, organized, and underserved—yet invisible to policy

Informal trade sustained the local economy

Traders were the backbone of daily life in Nyanga, filling critical gaps left by limited formal retail (one mall served nearly 58,000 people - census data at the time of this project).

Their goods and services—affordable, essential, and community-rooted—relied on informal supply chains that supported livelihoods well beyond the market itself.

Trade was structured, cooperative, and long-standing

For many, trading was their life long career. 52% of the 365 audited traders had worked from the same kiosk for over 11 years—some for more than 35.

Many stalls were family-run or cooperatively managed.

Trader groups were self-organized by product type, with systems in place for shared space, informal governance, and mutual support.

An analysis of grouping and arrangement: 365 Traders were mapped in detail (indicated in red). We discovered that they worked together to share resources, arranging their stalls optimise their interface with customers.

Infrastructure was absent, and working conditions unsafe
"There are lots of skollies (thieves)… We try to protect each other and hired a security guard together. We don’t have any storage—it’s a problem. Anything left in the stall at night gets stolen."
– Female meat trader, Nyanga

Traders needed support. They lacked access to storage, water, waste disposal, toilets, and lighting—affecting both dignity and income.

Theft and harassment were common, especially among women traders, who faced elevated risks. Crime hotspots were identified through co-mapping exercises with traders.

Policy did not reflect reality

Permitting regulations were designed for individual stall ownership—not catering to shared or cooperative setups. This disconnect led to unintended violations and conflict with law enforcement, who often confiscated goods when the permit holder wasn't present.

Small, uniformly sized rentable trading bays failed to match the diverse spatial and operational needs of different trader types. Realistically, these would be hard to implement.

A family sharing ownership and responsibility

Above all, traders feared relocation

The greatest concern was being moved. Relocation risked severing customer relationships and disrupting decades of established practices, working together.

The risks

An intervention insensitive to informal trade risked harming livelihoods and the broader ecosystem

It became clear that any top-down upgrade that failed to consider how informal trade truly functioned could do more harm than good—displacing traders, disrupting fragile supply chains, and undermining local economies.

Design response

Putting traders at the heart of the upgrade

Informed by field research and co-design sessions, we developed six core design principles to guide a trading plan that was inclusive, practical, and sensitive to local dynamics.

1. A phased upgrade prioritizing inclusion and continuity

  • Accommodate all existing traders in the plan and allow for future growth
  • Phase construction to allow continuous operation

2. Minimize relocation and protect livelihoods

  • Relocate only when essential (e.g. meat traders requiring hygiene services)
  • New locations should be co-designed to improve conditions, increase foot traffic, and support business growth

3. A central market as an anchor

  • Consolidate trade at the public transport interchange to maximize visibility
  • Provide infrastructure for food and meat traders within a shared cooperative space

Deliverables

A strategy grounded in lived experience

The final proposal included: A three-phase spatial upgrade A comprehensive trading plan A land release and maintenance strategy Detailed reports documenting research and stakeholder engagement While implementation stalled due to shifting city priorities, the design provided a precedent for inclusive urban development—showing that trade, safety, and dignity are not mutually exclusive goals.

Outcomes and lessons

Designing with people, not for them

Listening as learning

This was one of my earliest experiences in user-centered design, and it reshaped how I approach research and collaboration. The traders we worked with had deep, lived expertise—built over decades. No framework or formal process could match their understanding of daily operations and needs.

Working across cultural difference

Our team and the community came from different cultural backgrounds. This required more than “empathy”—it demanded curiosity, self-awareness, and humility. I learned to check my own assumptions and adapt how I communicated, researched, and collaborated.

Trust, hope—and hard questions

Despite our efforts to manage expectations, many traders became deeply engaged in the co-design process. They showed up, contributed, and hoped. And then—for nearly a decade—nothing changed. That experience taught me something that’s still with me today: When people share their time, insight, and trust, they’re doing more than participating in a process—they’re investing in a possible future. And that means designers carry a responsibility that extends beyond the proposal stage.

A delayed return

In 2021, the City of Cape Town returned to SUN Development to revisit the project and build on the work we produced. I was no longer with the team—but that decision showed the long-term value of the research and strategic foundations we had laid. Still, I’ve never forgotten what it felt like to leave a project where real people were left waiting.

From Insight to Inclusion: A Design Strategy for Informal Trade

Project background

In 2013, the Municipality of Cape Town identified a busy, but unsafe and under-resourced public transport hub in Nyanga for regeneration. As part of a non-profit consortium contracted to deliver an insight-driven urban design proposal, I worked as part of a multidisciplinary team to understand the needs of informal traders—a central but underserved group in the area. My work surfaced actionable insights that shaped a regeneration plan grounded in inclusion, safety, and economic resilience.

Why was an upgrade commissioned?

Public safety, unregulated trade, and 7,000 daily commuters: A high-pressure urban hub in need of a better system

Located in Nyanga, one of Cape Town’s most densely populated and economically challenged neighborhoods, the site faced persistent problems with crime, overcrowding, and unregulated informal trade. Hundreds of informal traders operated daily, playing a vital role in the local economy—but also contributing to sidewalk congestion and regulatory friction with the City. From the municipality’s perspective, this created a need for a safer, more organized public space that balanced enforcement with economic support. Our team, SUN Development, was commissioned to design a comprehensive regeneration plan, including a trading strategy that supported the informal economy rather than displacing it.

Project objective

The goal was to create a trading plan that supported—not displaced—informal traders.

Achieving this meant developing a deep understanding of how trade functioned, what traders needed, and how urban upgrades could help or harm their livelihoods. Our task was to design for inclusion, continuity, and long-term resilience.

The research process

Immersive fieldwork and multi-stakeholder engagement.

Our research combined contextual analysis, on-site observation, and deep engagement with traders and stakeholders. I contributed across all stages and led two key research components:

Understanding trade in context

We began with a review of municipal data to understand the area's socio-economic landscape and spatial systems.

Observational fieldwork:

As a team, we studied the daily rhythm of informal trade in relation to commuter flows, movement patterns, infrastructure, and friction points.

Stakeholder walkabouts:

We engaged with traders, residents, municipal officials, and law enforcement to hear firsthand accounts of the area’s challenges.

Gathering stories on site walkabouts

Interview notes

Leading research on trader operations and experiences

I led a focused investigation into how informal trade functioned on the ground, coordinating closely with two urban planning interns.

Mapping daily operations:

I guided the fieldwork process, working with the interns to map 365 trader stalls, capturing location, typology, operations, and spatial needs.

Designing and conducting interviews:

I developed interview scripts, selected a representative sample across trade types, and conducted 20 in-depth interviews with traders. I also led the analysis of findings, identifying key themes around business structure, pain points, and daily practices.

Data integration:

I worked with the team analyzing quantitative audit data (including geolocated stalls and demographics) to enrich our understanding and validate patterns.

Policy and precedent

Alongside the urban designer, I reviewed municipal by-laws impacting informal trade to understand how regulations aligned—or clashed—with reality. We also conducted a field visit to Warwick Junction, a successful example of a government–trader partnership, where we interviewed the implementation team and captured lessons learned.

Navigating complexity

Design under pressure: Trust, language, and institutional silos

Building trust:

Our link to the municipality initially created skepticism. Language barriers and the presence of police escorts added tension. We worked with local facilitators to build trust and ensure informed participation.

Inclusive communication:

Technical drawings alienated some participants, so we used site walkabouts, storytelling, and physical models to promote engagement and co-creation.

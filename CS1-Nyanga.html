From Insight to Inclusion: A Design Strategy for Informal Trade

Project background

In 2013, the Municipality of Cape Town identified a busy, but unsafe and under-resourced public transport hub in Nyanga for regeneration. As part of a non-profit consortium contracted to deliver an insight-driven urban design proposal, I worked as part of a multidisciplinary team to understand the needs of informal traders—a central but underserved group in the area. My work surfaced actionable insights that shaped a regeneration plan grounded in inclusion, safety, and economic resilience.

Why was an upgrade commissioned?

Public safety, unregulated trade, and 7,000 daily commuters: A high-pressure urban hub in need of a better system

Located in Nyanga, one of Cape Town’s most densely populated and economically challenged neighborhoods, the site faced persistent problems with crime, overcrowding, and unregulated informal trade. Hundreds of informal traders operated daily, playing a vital role in the local economy—but also contributing to sidewalk congestion and regulatory friction with the City. From the municipality’s perspective, this created a need for a safer, more organized public space that balanced enforcement with economic support. Our team, SUN Development, was commissioned to design a comprehensive regeneration plan, including a trading strategy that supported the informal economy rather than displacing it.

Project objective

The goal was to create a trading plan that supported—not displaced—informal traders.

Achieving this meant developing a deep understanding of how trade functioned, what traders needed, and how urban upgrades could help or harm their livelihoods. Our task was to design for inclusion, continuity, and long-term resilience.

The discovery

Trade was essential, organized, and underserved—yet invisible to policy

Informal trade sustained the local economy

Traders were the backbone of daily life in Nyanga, filling critical gaps left by limited formal retail (one mall served nearly 58,000 people - census data at the time of this project).

Their goods and services—affordable, essential, and community-rooted—relied on informal supply chains that supported livelihoods well beyond the market itself.

Trade was structured, cooperative, and long-standing

For many, trading was their life long career. 52% of the 365 audited traders had worked from the same kiosk for over 11 years—some for more than 35.

Many stalls were family-run or cooperatively managed.

Trader groups were self-organized by product type, with systems in place for shared space, informal governance, and mutual support.

Infrastructure was absent, and working conditions unsafe

Traders lacked access to storage, water, toilets, and lighting—affecting both dignity and income.

Theft and harassment were common, especially among women traders, who faced elevated risks. Crime hotspots were identified through co-mapping exercises.

"There are lots of skollies (thieves)… We try to protect each other and hired a security guard together. We don’t have any storage—it’s a problem. Anything left in the stall at night gets stolen." – Female trader, Nyanga

Policy did not reflect reality

Regulations were designed for formal, individual stall ownership—ignoring shared or cooperative setups.

This disconnect led to unintended violations and conflict with law enforcement.

Uniform bay sizes failed to match the diverse spatial and operational needs of different trader types.

Above all, traders feared relocation

The greatest concern was being moved. Relocation risked severing customer relationships and disrupting decades of established practices. Many had seen similar projects fail to accommodate them in the past.

The risks

Ignoring informal systems risked harm to livelihoods

It became clear that any top-down upgrade that failed to consider how informal trade truly functioned could do more harm than good—displacing traders, disrupting fragile supply chains, and undermining local economies.

Design response

Putting traders at the heart of the upgrade

Informed by field research and co-design sessions, we developed six core design principles to guide a trading plan that was inclusive, practical, and sensitive to local dynamics.

A phased upgrade prioritizing inclusion and continuity

Accommodate all existing traders in the plan and allow for future growth

Phase construction to allow continuous operation

Minimize relocation and protect livelihoods

Relocate only when essential (e.g. meat traders requiring hygiene services)

New locations should be co-designed to improve conditions, increase foot traffic, and support business growth

A central market as an anchor

Consolidate trade at the public transport interchange to maximize visibility

Provide infrastructure for food and meat traders within a shared cooperative space

Deliverables

A strategy grounded in lived experience

The final proposal included: A three-phase spatial upgrade A comprehensive trading plan A land release and maintenance strategy Detailed reports documenting research and stakeholder engagement While implementation stalled due to shifting city priorities, the design provided a precedent for inclusive urban development—showing that trade, safety, and dignity are not mutually exclusive goals.
